Arc Story · United States · 2005–2025

The Self-Reinforcing Security Spiral

US strategy toward China, 2006–2025. How an institutional ecology learned to produce the threat that justifies its own expansion.

Kari Freyr Epiphenomenon@Trove Neural Nations

American strategy toward China across the first quarter of the 21st century moved through three analytically distinct phases: an integrationist plateau (2001–2008) anchored in the "responsible stakeholder" doctrine; a pivotal and ambiguous rebalancing (2009–2016) under Obama; and a structural rupture (2017–2021) under Trump's first administration that reframed the entire bilateral relationship from cooperative engagement to existential strategic competition.

Putin's Munich speech of February 2007 sits at the hinge of this arc — an act of open naming that activated a Western security-discourse machinery already primed, and that provides the clearest single illustration of the thesis these essays advance: that in a structurally self-reinforcing security ecology, naming resistance generates the counter-narrative that retroactively erases the provocations that produced the resistance.

· · ·

The Bush–Zoellick Baseline, 2005–2008

The intellectual framework governing US–China policy in the mid-2000s was articulated by Deputy Secretary of State Robert Zoellick in his September 2005 speech urging China to become a "responsible stakeholder" in the US-led international order. The concept was deliberately constructed to contrast with Cold War containment: Zoellick explicitly distinguished the new framework from the Soviet-era policy of "fencing in" a rival, proposing instead that China's growing power be channelled toward sustaining the rules-based system that had enabled its own rise.

The doctrine had three constitutive features that matter for this analysis. First, it acknowledged China's success — including its success at partially escaping US tutelage — as a given. Second, it set behavioural benchmarks that Washington would use to judge whether Beijing was being "responsible," standards that were, of course, calibrated to US interests and institutional preferences. Third, and crucially, it maintained a parallel military hedge: despite the "engagement" rhetoric, the Pentagon simultaneously sustained a programme of alliance-strengthening, non-allied military partnerships surrounding China, and unilateral Pacific force build-up. The dualism — engagement in principle, hedging in practice — was visible in the 2006 Quadrennial Defense Review, which expressed hope that China would "emerge as a responsible stakeholder" while simultaneously expanding the military architecture designed to constrain it.

This structural contradiction — naming China a partner while building a containment perimeter — is precisely the precondition for the pattern the CAMS framework and Trove analysis identify: the security logic produces the conditions for the discourse that justifies its own expansion, while the actions generating those conditions remain outside the frame of named aggression.

The Munich Inflection Point: Putin, 2007

On 10 February 2007, Putin delivered his landmark address to the 43rd Munich Security Conference. He named what had not previously been named in such a direct public forum: that the United States had "overstepped its national borders in every sphere"; that the unipolar world order was itself destabilising; that NATO expansion contradicted assurances made at the Cold War's end; and that the planned US missile defence installations in Poland and the Czech Republic constituted an existential threat to Russia's nuclear deterrent. He proposed a counter-architecture — a counter-proposal to the missile shield was submitted to Bush in June 2007 and declined.

The Western response was diagnostic. The White House was "surprised and disappointed". Spokesman Tony Snow asserted that the Bush administration had "always begun with an international diplomatic component" — an assertion that erased, for example, the unilateral invasion of Iraq four years earlier. Senator Jon Kyl demanded a pledge against unilateral Russian military action. The speech was framed as aggression, as a return to Cold War thinking, as evidence of Russian hostility — not as a precise and documented description of actions taken, or as the kind of statement Kennedy himself had made in the Cuban missile crisis context about mutual security imperatives.

The resistance was named; the provocations generating the resistance were not. This is the Munich speech as a case study in what the Trove analysis identifies as a structural feature of the security ecology: the interventions are not described as aggression; the resistance to those interventions generates the Red Scare.

The Munich speech then became the functional pivot for the China trajectory as well. Western strategy after 2007 increasingly understood the Russia challenge and the China challenge as connected — that a failure to discipline Russia would embolden China, and vice versa. The speech activated a US security apparatus that was already primed, through the Zoellick hedge and the QDR, for a transition from selective engagement to competitive management.

The Obama Years — Pivot, Rebalance, and Epistemic Ambiguity, 2009–2016

Obama's East Asia strategy, formally announced in Hillary Clinton's 2011 Foreign Policy article and formalised in the 2012 "Pivot to Asia," represented the most significant structural realignment of US Pacific posture since the Cold War. Its six key action lines spanned the diplomatic, economic, and military axes: strengthening bilateral security alliances; deepening engagement with emerging powers including China; expanding military presence; forging multilateral economic institutions (primarily the Trans-Pacific Partnership); and advancing democracy and human rights.

The stated logic was hedging, not containment. Obama officials distinguished between "balancing" China's rise and opposing it, arguing the two were compatible. But the operational content told a different story. Washington expanded joint military exercises with allies for defence of disputed islands in the East and South China Seas. It restored defence cooperation with Vietnam, Indonesia, and New Zealand — nations that China understood as part of a strategic encirclement. Growing concerns about China's Anti-Access/Area Denial strategy led the Pentagon to issue in 2012 the Joint Operational Access Concept, establishing the Air-Sea Battle strategy specifically designed to defeat A2AD. Australia's Parliament was the venue chosen by Obama for the announcement of the Pivot in 2011 — a geopolitically deliberate choice that foregrounded the Five Eyes network as the sinew of the new architecture.

The pivot was a genuine strategic shift away from the Middle East and toward the management of China's rise as the primary challenge of US foreign policy. Yet the naming problem persisted. The administration consistently described the Pivot as not directed at China while deploying assets, strengthening alliances, and building operational doctrine whose only coherent target was China's military-strategic capacity. From Beijing's perspective — and by the analytic logic these essays apply to the Russia case — the symmetry was exact: the containment was real; the naming of it as containment was denied.

The Obama–China Paradox: Engagement and Discipline

The Obama administration simultaneously deepened economic interdependence with China — maintaining and expanding the Strategic and Economic Dialogue established under Bush — while building the military and diplomatic architecture designed to constrain Chinese options in its own region. This dualism was not accidental hypocrisy; it reflected a genuine strategic bet that deep economic integration would moderate Chinese behaviour and create the conditions for a stable great-power equilibrium. It was, in structural terms, the responsible-stakeholder doctrine with a harder military edge.

The bet failed on two fronts. China's behaviour in the South China Sea, particularly after 2012–2013, confounded the integration hypothesis: economic interdependence did not translate into restraint on territorial claims. And Obama's pivot reinforced Chinese elite conviction — already embedded after the 2008 financial crisis demonstrated US systemic fragility — that the United States was in managed decline and would resist China's rise regardless of Beijing's behaviour. The security dilemma tightened in both directions simultaneously.

CAMS reading — Early Scissors Effect
Shield-Craft rising · Archive-Lore under stress

From the CAMS-CAN framework, this period shows the early Scissors Effect in the US system: fast-loop security nodes (Shield, Craft) expanding and activated, while the slow-loop epistemic and deliberative nodes (Archive, Lore — the diplomatic, scholarly, and institutional infrastructure for genuine engagement with Chinese complexity) were already under stress. The discourse of competition was outpacing the institutional capacity for its management.

The Trump Rupture — From Management to Confrontation, 2017–2021

The 2017 NSS: Naming the Adversary

The December 2017 National Security Strategy constituted a doctrinal watershed — not in the underlying strategic logic, which had been building since 2007, but in the explicit naming. For the first time, a formal US strategy document described China and Russia as "revisionist powers" attempting to "erode American security and prosperity," challenging American power, influence, and interests. The label was significant precisely because it was a departure from the responsible-stakeholder and even pivot-era rhetorical frames: China was no longer a rising power to be managed within the international order, but a power seeking to overthrow that order.

The primary author, Nadia Schadlow, later noted the document was well-received across the foreign policy spectrum — a telling detail. The bipartisan consensus that the Obama and Trump administrations had, between them, constructed around the China-as-competitor framing was now formalised. The 2017 NSS established the doctrinal baseline that persisted through Biden and into Trump's second administration. What changed between administrations was the emphasis and the instruments, not the underlying competition paradigm.

The Internal Factions: Navarro, Bannon, and the Hawks

Trump's China policy was not monolithic. His administration contained at least two overlapping but analytically distinct China-hostile factions. The first, represented by Peter Navarro, Stephen Miller, and Steve Bannon, was economic nationalist: it sought to reverse what it saw as the hollowing-out of American manufacturing through decades of China-enabling trade policy, and aimed to speed the decoupling of the two economies. The second, represented by Vice President Pence and Secretary of State Pompeo, was ideological-civilisational: it framed the contest with China as a world-historical confrontation between freedom and totalitarianism, drawing explicitly on Cold War rhetorical architecture.

Chinese analysts at the time — accurately — identified the convergence of these three forces (economic nationalism, religious conservatism, ideological anti-communism) in a single administration as historically unprecedented since 1979. The convergence was not accidental: it reflected the absorption, into the executive branch, of a China critique that had been building in the congressional, think-tank, and military-industrial complex for a decade.

The Pence–Pompeo Speeches: Discourse as Warfare

The October 2018 Pence speech at the Hudson Institute and the July 2020 Pompeo speech at the Nixon Library are the two most important single-document expressions of the Trump administration's China doctrine — and the clearest illustrations of the thesis in operation.

Pence's speech went further than any previous official statement in naming China as a comprehensive competitor using "a whole-of-government approach — political, economic, and military tools, as well as propaganda" against the United States. It explicitly cited the "Made in China 2025" plan as evidence of Beijing's ambition to control 90% of the world's advanced industries. It alleged Chinese interference in US domestic politics. Chinese analysts described it as portending a "new Cold War," and noted that Beijing interpreted the speech — correctly — as indicating Washington had entered a phase that would require China to prepare for a prolonged multidimensional conflict.

Pompeo's Nixon Library speech, delivered against the backdrop of the COVID-19 pandemic, went further still: it explicitly buried engagement ("I come not to praise engagement but to bury it"), framed the Chinese Communist Party as an "ideological foe that must be vanquished," and cited the Soviet precedent as a template for forcing regime change. He deployed Reagan's Cold War dictum against China: "distrust and verify". The parallel with the structural pattern identified in this project is exact: the speech simultaneously expanded the security competition, framed China's resistance to that competition as inherent malevolence, and produced — for a domestic audience — the discourse that justified further escalation.

The Trade War: Instruments and Outcomes

Between July 2018 and August 2019, the United States announced tariffs on over $550 billion of Chinese products; China retaliated with tariffs on over $185 billion of US goods. The formal justification centred on intellectual property theft, forced technology transfer, market access restrictions, and currency manipulation. By the end of Trump's first term, the trade war was widely characterised by American media as a failure for the United States: it had not reduced the trade deficit with China, had raised costs for US consumers and businesses, had not returned manufacturing jobs to the American heartland, and had not produced the structural changes in Chinese economic governance sought by the administration.

The trade war was, however, successful in one domain: it institutionalised the competition paradigm. By imposing tariffs unilaterally, then accepting a Phase One agreement that left the structural issues unresolved, the first Trump administration established a framework of managed hostility — with economic instruments as the primary field of contest — that the Biden administration largely continued and that the second Trump administration has expanded further.

The Australian Vector — Coordinated Provocation and the Coercion Framing

The shift in US China strategy during the Trump years did not occur in a bilateral vacuum. The Five Eyes intelligence alliance was progressively adapted from its original terrorism/state-warfare focus toward a China-specific strategic function. Australia's policy shift toward China during 2017–2022 is extensively documented as occurring in close alignment with the US strategic repositioning.

The critical moment was 2020. When Beijing concluded that Canberra was coordinating with Washington in a political attack over the COVID-19 pandemic's origins, it responded with trade restrictions on Australian barley, wine, beef, coal, cotton, and lobsters. The Australian narrative — which became the dominant frame in Anglosphere media — described these restrictions as "economic coercion": China was weaponising trade for political ends. The Australian Strategic Policy Institute and like-minded institutions amplified this frame consistently.

What the dominant narrative systematically omitted — and what the Trove analysis makes structurally legible — was the prior Australian coordination with US security actors that constituted, from Beijing's perspective, the provocation. The sequence was: Australian–US security coordination; Chinese trade restrictions; Australian–Anglosphere media framing of restrictions as unprovoked coercion. The coordination that preceded the restrictions was not described as provocation; the restrictions were described as coercion. This is the pattern the Trove analysis identifies across 105 years: the interventions are erased; the resistance generates the Red Scare.

This is not to argue that China's trade actions were proportionate or justified — only to note that the dominant discourse produced by the security ecology was structurally asymmetric in its accounting of causes.

The Epistemic Cost

The Five Eyes' progressive blurring of intelligence function and policy function — warned against by the Lowy Institute as recently as March 2026 — illustrates the epistemic degradation the CAMS-CAN framework formally models. When intelligence agencies become policy advocates for a particular framing of the threat (China as inherently malevolent), they degrade their own capacity for the truth-to-power function that distinguishes intelligence from propaganda. The Archive and Lore nodes — in CAMS terms, the institutional repositories of objective information and analytical rigour — are subordinated to the Shield function. The Library Attractor weakens even as fast-loop security reactivity intensifies.

A system in which security logic produces the discourse that justifies its own expansion, while simultaneously degrading the epistemic institutions that might contest it.

The Structural Pattern — Applying the Thesis

The Munich Speech as Template

Putin's 2007 Munich speech is the cleanest available case study for the thesis because it was a speech: a documented, public, precisely argued act. Putin did not invade; he named. He named NATO expansion, the missile defence installations, the broken assurances, the unilateral military interventions, the manipulation of international institutions. The Western reaction was to treat the naming as the threatening act — "surprised and disappointed" — while the actions named remained outside the category of provocation.

Within eighteen months, the US-Russia security spiral had intensified markedly: the Russo-Georgian War of 2008, Washington's support for the Saakashvili government, the accelerated Eastern European NATO discussions, and the effective end of the Bush-era cooperative security architecture. The Munich speech activated the security apparatus not because it was wrong in its factual claims (subsequent scholarship has largely validated its account of NATO expansion assurances) — but because it violated the discursive rules of the security ecology: the actions of the dominant power are not named as aggression; only the resistance to those actions is named as aggression.

The China analogue runs exactly parallel. From Zoellick's 2005 speech through the Pivot to the Pence and Pompeo speeches, US strategy progressively named China as a threat while framing its own actions as defensive or rule-based. China's responses — South China Sea assertiveness, Made in China 2025, BRI, Wolf Warrior diplomacy — were named as aggressive; the military encirclement that produced them was not. The discourse of Chinese aggression justified the security build-up that made Chinese assertiveness rational, which in turn justified further build-up.

The Ideological Rupture Under Trump

The Obama pivot maintained the structural ambiguity: engagement and hedging coexisted, and the responsible-stakeholder aspiration was never formally abandoned. The Trump first administration abolished this ambiguity. The 2017 NSS, the Pence speech, and the Pompeo speech each in different ways closed the deliberative space for a different framing.

CAMS reading — Type III ecology
Helm captured · Archive subordinated · Shield-Craft self-directing

This is the Type III ecology the CAMS research describes: a system in which the Helm node has been captured by security imperatives, Archive is subordinated, Stewards-node legitimacy is maintained through threat inflation, and the fast-loop Shield-Craft complex has become self-directing. The structural self-reinforcement is not a conspiracy; it is a phase transition. Once the discourse of existential competition is institutionalised — in strategy documents, in intelligence cooperation, in Five Eyes policy alignment, in bipartisan congressional consensus — the cost of contesting it exceeds the institutional capacity of the epistemic nodes that might do so.

Whether the Type III Ecology Is Reversible

The analytical question this framing raises — whether the Type III ecology can transition back to Type I or II — is not answerable from the strategic record reviewed here, and the CAMS research is correct that it is not answerable from the dataset. What the record does show is the following.

The transition from Type I/II to Type III was not inevitable; it was the product of specific choices, specific speeches, specific institutional decisions at identifiable moments: Zoellick's hedging architecture, the Munich speech's activation of the security machinery, the Obama Pivot's military operationalisation, the Trump administration's ideological weaponisation, the Five Eyes expansion. These were decisions, not fate.

The 2025 National Security Strategy under Trump's second administration actually introduces an ambiguity that the first administration did not: it removes explicit great-power competition language, frames the China challenge almost exclusively in economic terms, and avoids the ideological framing of the Pence-Pompeo era. Whether this represents a genuine recalibration or a rhetorical shift concealing strategic continuity remains unclear. What Brookings analysts note is that China is not losing the economic competition with the United States, and that the "small yard, big fence" technology restriction strategy has not arrested Chinese technological advance in critical sectors.

Structural risk · 2025
Helm 3.0 · Archive 1.5 · Scissors Effect confirmed

The CAMS data confirms the structural risk: the United States in 2025 shows Helm suppressed at 3.0, Archive critically low at 1.5, and a clear Scissors Effect — fast-loop Craft-Shield reactivity elevated against declining slow-loop deliberative capacity. This is the measurable signature of a system that cannot easily transition back to deliberative mode, not because the knowledge does not exist, but because the institutional capacity to deploy it against the security consensus has been progressively eroded. Lore, as the Germany and Rome cases show, is the last node to fail — but it, too, degrades if the fast-loop decoupling persists.

The analytically and practically urgent question is whether the Lore-Archive coupling can be restored before the Stewards-node legitimacy floor is breached — whether, in strategic terms, the epistemic institutions that might contest the security discourse can be rebuilt faster than the security discourse completes its self-reinforcing cycle.

Systemic Reflection

First Principles

Self-reinforcing security ecologies are not conspiracies. They are phase transitions in complex adaptive systems: once a threshold of institutional consensus is crossed, the cost of contesting the dominant frame exceeds the capacity of the epistemic nodes that might do so. The 2017 NSS, the Pence and Pompeo speeches, and the Five Eyes alignment did not produce a strategic situation; they produced a discourse that produced the strategic situation.

Core Wisdom

Across 105 years of comparable cases — allied intervention in Russia, NATO expansion through 2007, the Pivot, the Australian–US 2020 coordination — the discursive asymmetry is consistent: the actions of the dominant power are not named as aggression; only the resistance to those actions is. This is the structural signature of the self-reinforcing security ecology, and it is legible in the discourse data long before it is legible in conventional strategic analysis.

Leverage Points

The Lore-Archive coupling is the load-bearing institutional structure. When intelligence agencies become policy advocates, when scholarly institutions absorb the security consensus, when journalism becomes alignment-checking — the Archive collapses and Lore is captured. Restoring that coupling is the precondition for any meaningful re-deliberation of the China relationship; restoring it later than the Stewards legitimacy floor is breached is the precondition for catastrophe.

Stakeholder Resonances

This analysis is relevant to policymakers asking whether US-China strategy is evidence-driven or system-driven; to intelligence analysts trying to preserve the distinction between assessment and advocacy; to scholars and journalists working under institutional pressure to align with the dominant frame; and to citizens of both Five Eyes and Chinese systems navigating a great-power confrontation whose discursive architecture has progressively foreclosed the deliberative options that might have produced different outcomes.
