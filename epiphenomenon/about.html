§ 1

What is Trove?

In the basement of the National Library of Australia, at least metaphorically, lives one of the most remarkable historical datasets in the world. Trove is the Library's digitised newspaper archive — a continuously growing collection of Australian newspapers, periodicals, and ephemera stretching from the colonial era to the mid-twentieth century. By the time this study was conducted, it contained more than 55 million digitised documents spanning over 1,800 newspaper titles.

What makes Trove exceptional for research is not merely its size but its searchability. The library's optical character recognition program, supplemented by millions of hours of volunteer human correction, has rendered the archive machine-readable at a level of fidelity sufficient for quantitative analysis. You can search for a term — "China", "Bolshevik", "Yellow Peril" — and receive not just the number of mentions but the raw count and the corpus size, from which a normalised frequency can be computed.

That normalised frequency — mentions per million words, or ppm — is the primary measure used in this study. It converts raw counts into rates that are comparable across years despite the enormous variation in total corpus size (a year with many newspapers in the archive will naturally produce more raw mentions than a year with few, regardless of any underlying trend in how often the topic was discussed).

Trove — key figures
55M+

Documents indexed in the Trove digitised press archive. The corpus spans approximately 1803 to the present, with densest coverage for 1880–1955. Per-million-word normalisation allows meaningful comparison across years with radically different corpus sizes — from 2.5M words in 1900 to 4.5M in the 1930s peak.

The critical advantage Trove offers for this kind of study is that it provides an independent, unplanned historical record. The newspapers that Australians were reading in 1905 were not written with any awareness that they would one day be analysed for keyword frequency. They are, in the strongest sense, evidence of what Australian public discourse was actually occupied with — as distinct from what historians have subsequently said it was occupied with, or what politicians of the day said it should be occupied with.

This independence from retrospective framing is precisely what makes Trove valuable as a cross-validation instrument for the CAMS structural analysis. If the structural model says Australia was experiencing Shield node intensification in a given period, and the Trove frequency data independently shows a spike in threat-referential language in that same period, the convergence is meaningful. Two different measurement systems, neither aware of the other, pointing to the same phenomenon.

The study uses Trove data for the period 1900–1954, supplemented by the Australian Parliamentary Hansard for 2006–2025. The Hansard data fills a different function: where Trove captures mass public discourse, Hansard captures elite political discourse. Together they allow us to ask whether what was happening in the press was tracking what was happening in parliament — and whether either was tracking the underlying structural CAMS signal.

§ 2

Why we designed the Trove experiment

A structural model that produces only structural outputs faces an inherent validation problem. How do you know it is measuring something real, rather than something the framework has constructed? CAMS scores institutional structure; but institutional structure is not directly observable. You cannot point to the Shield node and measure it the way you can measure a country's GDP or its military spending. The score is an interpretive synthesis of many indicators, weighted according to theoretical assumptions about how complex adaptive systems work.

The Trove experiment was designed to address this validation problem from an unexpected angle. The hypothesis was simple: if Shield hypertrophy is a real structural phenomenon with real consequences for a society's institutional life, it should produce an observable footprint in public discourse. Specifically, it should produce elevated threat-referential language — because a society whose security apparatus has grown to dominate its knowledge-memory complex is precisely the kind of society that generates, sustains, and amplifies threat narratives.

Core hypothesis

H1 (Structural–discursive coupling): Periods of elevated CAMS Shield node activity should correlate with elevated sino-referential and russo-referential discourse frequency in the Trove press archive, after controlling for geopolitical events that independently drive both.

H2 (Discourse–structure lag): The direction of the coupling is not assumed — the experiment is designed to test whether structural change precedes discourse change (structure drives narrative) or discourse change precedes structural change (narrative drives structure), or whether they are co-constitutive.

H3 (Enemy cycling): If the structural machinery is the primary driver, then when one threat discourse recedes, another should rise to replace it — because the machinery requires an output, not a specific content. The Trove data allows this to be tested across the full 1900–1954 period.

The design is powerful because Trove data is entirely exogenous to CAMS. The newspapers in the Trove archive were published without any awareness of CAMS or its categories. The discourse frequency data is not produced by the model; it is produced by the historical record. If the two systems converge — if elevated Shield node scores align with elevated threat discourse frequency — that convergence cannot be explained as a tautology. It is evidence of something real.

There is a second, more political reason the experiment was designed the way it was. The current period of Australian strategic anxiety — the contemporary China Threat discourse — is generating real policy consequences: AUKUS submarines, defence spending escalation, decoupling from trade relationships, hardening of intelligence alliances. These decisions are being made on the basis of a threat assessment whose historical precedents are poorly understood. The Trove experiment allows us to ask: has Australia been here before? And if so, what happened?

The answer — documented across 125 years of press data — is that Australia has indeed been here before, multiple times, with different named enemies. That finding does not prove the current threat assessment is wrong. But it does provide essential context for evaluating it: a context that conventional strategic analysis, which does not have access to long-run quantitative discourse data, cannot supply.

§ 3

What we were looking for

The study was not designed to prove a thesis. It was designed to test one — and to let the datasets speak for themselves in either direction. That said, the hypotheses that motivated the design were specific enough that it is worth stating them plainly.

The enemy cycling hypothesis

The primary question driving the Trove experiment was whether Australian threat discourse follows an institutional rhythm independent of the actual threat environment. If the machinery of threat production is real — if there is something about Australia's structural configuration that generates, sustains, and eventually replaces threat narratives on a regular cycle — then the Trove data should show: (a) multiple distinct threat cycles over 125 years, each peaking and then receding; (b) some evidence that when one threat recedes, another rises to replace it; and (c) cycle amplitudes that are not simply correlated with the magnitude of the external threat (because if they were, the external threat would be doing all the explanatory work).

The data broadly supports this hypothesis. The Yellow Peril cycle peaks in 1904–1905, recedes, is replaced by the Russian/Bolshevik cycle in the late 1910s, which itself recedes and is replaced by Cold War Russian discourse from 1948, which then gives way to the China Threat cycle from approximately 2017. The amplitudes vary; the structural rhythm is consistent.

The lead-time hypothesis

The secondary question — pursued most systematically in the Without Hindcasting working paper — was whether CAMS structural signals provide actionable lead time before events that, in retrospect, appear to have been structurally prepared for years in advance. The without-hindcasting discipline was designed to answer this as cleanly as possible.

The result — that Germany's robust SHI signal in 1935 provides a four-year lead time to war outbreak, surviving even the most pessimistic scorer interpretation — is the study's strongest quantitative finding. It does not prove that a 1935 analyst using CAMS would necessarily have drawn the right policy conclusions. It proves that the structural information necessary to draw those conclusions was present and measurable, years before the events that made it visible through conventional analysis.

The discourse-structure coupling question

The third question — probably the most important theoretically — is whether discourse and structure are coupled, and if so in which direction. Does Shield hypertrophy produce threat discourse, or does threat discourse produce Shield hypertrophy? Or, as the complexity framework suggests, are they entangled in a feedback loop where causation runs in both directions simultaneously?

The data does not definitively answer this question, which is itself a finding. What it shows is that the two systems are correlated, that the correlation is not explained by external events alone, and that in the Australian case the structural shift (toward positive SHI from approximately 2015) slightly precedes the most intense period of parliamentary China Threat discourse (2018–2021). But the margins are narrow, the methods are imperfect, and the honest conclusion is: the coupling is real, its direction is contested, and further research with better data would be valuable.

We were looking for the structural skeleton beneath the surface of events. We found it — not perfectly, not completely, but legibly enough to be useful. The datasets speak; the task is to listen without knowing in advance what they are going to say.

A note on what we were not looking for

This study is not an argument that Australia faces no genuine strategic challenges in its relationship with China or Russia. The CAMS framework does not distinguish between manufactured and genuine threats — it measures structural configuration, not the accuracy of threat assessments. A country can have real enemies and still manufacture additional ones through the institutional dynamics described here. Both things can be true simultaneously, and the data cannot separate them.

What the study does argue — and what the 125-year Trove record makes hard to avoid — is that the structural machinery matters as much as the external stimulus. Understanding that machinery, measuring it, and making its outputs visible is the precondition for any genuinely evidence-based assessment of whether the current strategic moment is as unprecedented as it is routinely claimed to be.
